Lecture Recommendations

I have enjoyed the following lectures and can recommend them to anyone interested in the field. Some are specific to the universities I have attended but others are available online. (Yes, I heard multiple lecture series about the same topics.)
  • Linear Algebra - The Linear Algebra lectures by Gilbert Strang are a must for anyone interested in machine learning. He spends significant time per topic to build up an unmatched intuition.
  • Topology & Geometry - This lecture series by Tadashi Tokieda is one of my absolute favorites. Along the way of giving an introduction to the field, he guides you through the beauty of visual problem solving. Remember to draw pictures!
  • Computer Vision - My favorite publicly available lecture series about Computer Vision comes from Andreas Geiger. It gives a good introduction into the field and touches upon more recent topics as well.
  • Big Data - This lecture from Ghislain Fourny at ETH Zurich is just a pleasure to follow for anyone interested in Big Data. It covers the core topics for storing, retrieving, and manipulating large amounts of data.
  • Operations Research - For me, this was the most fun lecture during my time in Aachen. Marco Lübbecke and his team did a great job in combining theory with practical application of the learned problem modeling techniques. Even the homework was fun!

Publications

  • Do Efficient Transformers Really Save Computation?

    TL;DR: We study the class of Linear and Sparse Transformers in a CoT setting and find that to keep up with regular Transformers, their hidden dimension needs to scale with the problem size. On the other hand we identify a simple criterion which allows efficient Transformers to be more efficient.

    ICML, 2024

    Kai Yang, Jan Ackermann, Zhenyu He, Guhao Feng, Bohang Zhang, Yunzhen Feng, Qiwei Ye, Di He, Liwei Wang

    Paper

  • Maskomaly: Zero-shot Mask Anomaly Segmentation

    TL;DR: We show that pretrained Mask-based segmentation models can predict anomalies without further tuning. Additionally, we introduce a metric for anomaly segmentation that favors models with confident predictions.

    BMVC, 2023 (oral)

    Jan Ackermann, Christos Sakaridis, Fisher Yu
